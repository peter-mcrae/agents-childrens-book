# Chapter 1 — Wendy and the Tiny Helper

---

**1.**
Wendy was the General Counsel.
That meant she was the lawyer for a whole big company.
Her job was to read things.
Lots and lots of things.

*Illustration:* Wendy at a tidy wooden desk, reading glasses on, a pen in hand, a calm but slightly weary smile. A nameplate on her desk reads "General Counsel." Soft morning light. The office looks normal — for now.

---

**2.**
Every morning, contracts piled up on Wendy's desk.
Vendor contracts. Customer contracts. NDAs. Renewals.
The pile was so tall, it bumped the ceiling.
"I'll never read them all," sighed Wendy.

*Illustration:* The same desk, but now a teetering tower of papers rises all the way up and folds against the ceiling. Wendy is small at the base of it, holding up one tiny page, dwarfed. A houseplant on the desk leans away from the pile, alarmed.

---

**3.**
One day, a tiny glowing creature appeared on her desk.
"Hello!" it said. "I'm an agent. I can help."
Wendy squinted. "An agent? Like a spy?"
The creature laughed.
"No — I'm a little helper. I can read, think, and do things. But I only know what you teach me."

*Illustration:* A small, round, glowing sprite — amber-teal, big friendly eyes, no clear arms or legs yet — perched on the edge of Wendy's coffee mug. Wendy is leaning in, curious, glasses pushed up. The contract pile is still there in the background but a little less menacing.

---

**4.**
"How are you different from a regular computer program?" Wendy asked.
"A regular program does the same thing every time.
I read what's in front of me, decide what to do, try it, and check if it worked.
If it didn't, I try again."
"Oh!" said Wendy. "Like a junior associate."
"Exactly like that."

*Illustration:* A split scene. On one side: a stiff, gray box labeled "PROGRAM" running on rails like a windup toy. On the other side: the glowing sprite, looking at a piece of paper with a thoughtful expression, a little thought-bubble showing a question mark turning into a checkmark.

---

**5.**
"First," said the agent, "you need to tell me my job."
Wendy thought for a moment.
"Your job is to read every vendor contract that comes in. Find anything risky. And tell me about it."
"Got it!" said the agent.
"That's called my **system prompt** — my job description."

*Illustration:* Wendy at a typewriter or laptop, typing. The words she types appear in the air above the agent like a glowing scroll: *"Read every vendor contract. Find anything risky. Tell Wendy."* The agent reads the scroll with delight, like it just got handed a name tag.

---

**6.**
"But how will you *read* the contracts?" Wendy asked.
"I need hands!" said the agent.
"Hands are called **tools**.
Give me a tool to open the inbox.
A tool to read PDFs.
A tool to check our risky-clause list.
And a tool to write you a note."
"Done," said Wendy.

*Illustration:* The agent now has four little glowing arms, each holding a different object: an envelope (inbox), a PDF page, a checklist scroll, and a sticky note with a pencil. The arms are clearly "added on" — they could come off if she took them away.

---

**7.**
The agent got to work.
It opened a contract. *(Read.)*
It thought: "This vendor wants 90-day payment terms. Our rule says 30." *(Think.)*
It checked the risky-clause list. *(Use a tool.)*
It wrote Wendy a note: "This one needs your eyes." *(Use another tool.)*
Then it picked up the next contract and started again.
This was called **the loop**.

*Illustration:* A circular diagram drawn in the style of a children's-book chalkboard: four small scenes arranged in a ring with arrows between them — READ, THINK, ACT, CHECK — and the agent travels around the ring with a little trail of sparkles. The contract pile in the background is visibly shorter.

---

**8.**
By lunchtime, the agent had read forty contracts.
Wendy looked at the notes stacked neatly on her desk.
"Most of these are fine!" she said.
"And the risky ones — you found them all."
The agent beamed.

*Illustration:* Wendy holding a small, tidy stack of sticky notes, one eyebrow raised in pleasant surprise. A clock on the wall says noon. The agent glows a little brighter, hovering proudly. A sandwich sits half-eaten on the desk — she finally had time to eat lunch.

---

**9.**
But not everything was perfect.
One day, the agent flagged a contract that was totally fine.
"Why did you flag this?" asked Wendy.
"It mentioned *indemnify*," said the agent. "I thought that was always risky."
"Sometimes it is. Sometimes it isn't. Let me show you the difference."
Wendy added a new rule to the agent's job description.
The agent got better.
This was called an **eval** — checking the agent's work and teaching it.

*Illustration:* Wendy sitting beside the agent like a patient teacher, pointing at a contract together. A speech bubble from Wendy says "*Indemnify* isn't always risky — look at *which side* is doing the indemnifying." The agent's eyes are wide, learning. A little progress bar above its head ticks up one notch.

---

**10.**
Another day, the agent did something Wendy hadn't expected.
It tried to email a vendor directly to ask a question!
"Whoa, whoa!" said Wendy. "Let's only email people when I say so."
She added a **guardrail**: a little fence around what the agent could do.
Now the agent would ask Wendy first, and Wendy would decide.

*Illustration:* The agent, embarrassed, holding a half-sent email that's still floating in midair. Wendy, calm but firm, is drawing a small picket fence on the desk between the agent and the outbox. A little gate in the fence is labeled "Ask Wendy first." The agent nods.

---

**11.**
Soon, Wendy's pile of contracts was no longer scary.
The agent did the first read.
Wendy did the thinking that needed a real lawyer.
Together, they got more done than Wendy ever had, all by herself.

*Illustration:* The office is calmer. The contract pile is now a normal-sized stack. Wendy is leaning back in her chair with a coffee, actually reading one contract carefully. The agent is happily working through its own little pile beside her, sticky notes flying.

---

**12.**
"You know what?" said Wendy.
"I could build *another* one."
"Another agent?" said the tiny helper.
"Yes! One for tracking new laws. One for answering questions from our salespeople. One for reminding me when renewals are coming up."
"Build away," said the agent. "We're easier than people think."

*Illustration:* Wendy at a whiteboard, sketching out three more little glowing sprites with name tags: "Law-Watcher," "Sales Helper," "Renewal Reminder." The original agent watches with a grin, hovering by her shoulder like a co-conspirator.

---

## The End of Chapter 1

**Three things to remember:**
1. An agent is a helper that **reads, thinks, and acts** — in a loop.
2. You give it a **job** (system prompt) and **hands** (tools).
3. You **check its work** and put **fences** where it shouldn't wander.

*That's the whole thing.*
