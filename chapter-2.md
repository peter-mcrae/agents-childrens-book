# Chapter 2 — The Helper Gets Helpers

---

**1.**
Wendy's tiny helper was famous now.
Word had spread around the whole company.
Everyone wanted one.

*Illustration:* A line of people from different departments forms a friendly queue outside Wendy's office door — a marketer with a stack of NDAs, an HR manager with offer letters, a salesperson waving a contract, a finance lead with a calendar. The agent peeks out the door, a little overwhelmed but flattered.

---

**2.**
The marketing team wanted help with NDAs.
The HR team wanted help with offer letters.
The sales team had questions about every contract they sent.
And the finance team wanted to know when contracts were running out.
The helper said yes to all of it.

*Illustration:* The agent juggling four different documents in midair — an NDA, an offer letter, a sales contract, a calendar. Its little glowing body is stretched in four directions, like a starfish that's trying very hard.

---

**3.**
But something was going wrong.
The helper started mixing things up.
It told the sales team a marketing rule by mistake.
It looked tired (if a glowing creature can look tired).

*Illustration:* The agent slumped on the corner of Wendy's desk, glow dimmer than usual. Above its head, four thought-bubbles overlap and tangle into a knot. A salesperson in the doorway is reading a sticky note with a puzzled face — it clearly says the wrong thing.

---

**4.**
"I think I'm trying to know too many things at once," said the helper.
"I keep forgetting which job I'm doing."

*Illustration:* Close-up on the helper, looking up at Wendy with a small, honest expression. Behind it, a chalkboard is covered in too many overlapping rules — vendor rules crossed out over HR rules crossed out over sales rules. A mess.

---

**5.**
Wendy nodded.
"When a job gets too big, lawyers don't do it all themselves.
They build a team.
Each person on the team is great at *one* thing."

*Illustration:* Wendy at her whiteboard, drawing a simple organizational chart of stick figures — one at the top, three underneath. Above the chart she's written "TEAMS." The agent watches, intrigued.

---

**6.**
"Could *I* have a team?" asked the helper.
"You can," said Wendy.
"We'll call them **sub-agents**.
Little helpers — for you."

*Illustration:* The agent's eyes widen with wonder. Wendy is gesturing with both hands like a magician revealing the next trick. Three faint, blank sprite-shapes hover in the air around the agent, waiting to be filled in.

---

**7.**
So Wendy built three new helpers.
Each one was a specialist.

- **Clause-Checker** read contracts and looked for risky clauses. Just that.
- **Law-Watcher** read the news every morning for new laws. Just that.
- **Renewal-Reminder** kept a calendar of all the contracts, and pinged Wendy a month before each one ran out. Just that.

*Illustration:* A character-sheet page like the cast list at the start of a play. Three new sprites, each a slightly different color: Clause-Checker (deep blue, holding a magnifying glass), Law-Watcher (leafy green, holding a newspaper), Renewal-Reminder (warm orange, holding a tiny calendar). Each has a one-line job description under its name.

---

**8.**
Each specialist had its own job description.
Its own tools.
Its own little fences.
And each one only knew what it needed to know.

*Illustration:* Three small rooms side by side, like a dollhouse cutaway. Each room is tidy and contains exactly one sprite, the tools it needs, and a fence around it. No clutter. No extras.

---

**9.**
Now when something came in, the main helper looked at it and thought:
"Who is the right helper for this?"
Then it asked the right specialist.
The specialist did its job, and reported back.

*Illustration:* A flowchart in picture-book style. A new contract comes in the top. The main agent in the middle looks at it, then a glowing arrow points to Clause-Checker, who does the work and sends a tidy report back up. The other two specialists wait patiently in their rooms.

---

**10.**
"This is so much better," said the helper.
"I don't have to know everything.
I just have to know *who knows*."

*Illustration:* The main agent, glow back to full brightness, sitting comfortably at a small desk like a project manager. Three little intercom buttons on the desk, one for each specialist. A coffee cup. A small smile.

---

**11.**
"That's how good teams work," said Wendy.
"And good agents, too."

*Illustration:* Wendy and the main agent side by side, looking at the team of four (main + three specialists) arranged like a family photo. Everyone is glowing in their own color. Wendy looks proud — the way a mentor looks at a team coming into its own.

---

**12.**
Soon the team was humming.
Contracts got read.
Laws got watched.
Renewals got reminded.
And Wendy did the thinking only Wendy could do.

*Illustration:* A wide shot of the office, busy in a calm way. Each specialist works on its own task in its own corner. The main agent moves between them, coordinating. Wendy, at her desk in the foreground, is reading one important contract slowly and carefully — the kind of work only a real lawyer can do. The afternoon light is golden.

---

## The End of Chapter 2

**Three more things to remember:**
1. When one agent gets confused, give it teammates — **sub-agents**.
2. Each sub-agent does **one thing** well, with its own job, tools, and fences.
3. The main agent's job becomes knowing **who to ask**.
